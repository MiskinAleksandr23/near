# Rectangular SNC coefficients with arbitrary constant nodes

Let `k` be algebraically closed of characteristic zero and put

```text
R=k[x,u,v,w],
a(u)=product_(i=1)^r (u-alpha_i)^(m_i),
b(v)=product_(j=1)^s (v-beta_j)^(n_j),
f=a(u)b(v)x+G(u,v,w),
R=B[f],
```

where the `alpha_i` and the `beta_j` are pairwise distinct and all
`m_i,n_j` are positive.  Scalar factors in `a,b` are harmless.  This note
closes the part left open by the nonconstant-grid argument: the values

```text
h_ij(w)=G(alpha_i,beta_j,w)
```

are allowed to be constant at an arbitrary subset of the grid.

## Theorem

Under the hypotheses above, `B=k^[3]` and `f` is a coordinate of `R`.
The multiplicities `m_i,n_j` are completely arbitrary.

The new point is that constant nodes cannot occur in an arbitrary pattern.
The cylinder hypothesis forces, after perhaps interchanging `u` and `v`,
a subset `I` of the vertical components such that

```text
h_ij is constant  iff  i belongs to I.                 (1)
```

Thus the constant nodes are whole parallel rows.  The restrictions of
`G` to the coefficient divisor then have enough linearity to run the same
two-stage affine-fibration argument as for `u^m v^n`.

## 1. Facts supplied by all parallel levels

For every `c in k`, let

```text
X_c=Spec R/(f-c).
```

Translation in the polynomial variable of `B[f]` identifies `X_c` with
`Spec B`.  Hence every `X_c` is smooth and factorial, has units `k*`, and
has compactly supported Euler characteristic one.

Write

```text
g_i(v,w)=G(alpha_i,v,w),
q_j(u,w)=G(u,beta_j,w).
```

For a generic `c`, every `g_i-c` and `q_j-c` is irreducible and reduced.
Indeed, the usual divisor argument is applied after inverting `ab`:

```text
(R/(f-c))_(ab)=k[u,v,(ab)^(-1),w].                    (2)
```

Its units modulo `k*` are freely generated by the `r+s` reduced factors
`u-alpha_i,v-beta_j`.  A factor of (say) `g_i-c` gives a height-one prime
over `u-alpha_i`.  Factoriality makes it principal, and comparison of its
valuation with the unit lattice in (2) forces one factor of exponent one,
provided `c` is not a constant node value.  A generic `c` avoids the
finitely many such values.  Generic smoothness then makes the curves

```text
C_i=V(g_i-c),       E_j=V(q_j-c)                     (3)
```

smooth and irreducible.

Let `E_0` be the set of grid nodes at which `h_ij` is constant.  At a
nonconstant node put `d_ij=deg h_ij`.  Inclusion-exclusion on the reduced
coefficient divisor, together with `chi_c(X_c)=1`, gives

```text
sum_i delta_i + sum_j epsilon_j
  + sum_((i,j) notin E_0) (d_ij-1) = #E_0,           (4)

delta_i=1-chi(C_i),        epsilon_j=1-chi(E_j).
```

Here every delta and epsilon is nonnegative: for a smooth irreducible
affine curve of genus `g` with `q` places at infinity it equals
`2g+q-1`.

We will also use the all-level boundary lattice.  For every `c`, the
localization exact sequence and (2) identify

```text
Z^(r+s)=<u-alpha_i,v-beta_j>
   --> direct_sum_(P subset V(ab), ht P=1) Z[P]       (5)
```

by an isomorphism.  In particular there are exactly `r+s` boundary
primes, and their valuation matrix is unimodular.  At a constant node of
value `c`, the prime

```text
P_ij=(u-alpha_i,v-beta_j) in R/(f-c)                 (6)
```

is a single boundary prime shared by the two branches.  Formula (5) is
the coupled, rather than branchwise, divisor calculation needed at such a
node.

## 2. Constant pencils and the orientation lemma

Call a vertical vertex `i` **inactive** if the projection `v:C_i->A1_v`
is constant on the geometric generic fiber; define inactive horizontal
vertices symmetrically.

If `i` is inactive, then

```text
g_i(v,w)=lambda_i v+mu_i,       lambda_i!=0.          (7)
```

Indeed constancy on the generic fiber says that `g_i` factors through
`v`.  Thus `g_i=P(v)`, and generic irreducibility of `P(v)-c` forces
`deg P=1`.  Conversely, (7) is plainly inactive.  In particular every
node incident with an inactive vertex is constant.

If `i` is active and exactly `t_i` of its incident nodes are constant,
then

```text
delta_i >= t_i.                                      (8)
```

On `C_i` the `t_i` distinct functions `v-beta_j` are units.  Their
classes modulo constants are independent: a multiplicative relation
would be a rational-function identity among distinct linear factors in
the nonconstant function `v`.  Therefore the generic curve has at least
`t_i+1` places at infinity, which proves (8).  The horizontal assertion
is identical.

There cannot be an inactive vertical vertex and an inactive horizontal
vertex simultaneously.  If they met at `(alpha_i,beta_j)` with value
`gamma`, then at level `gamma` equations (7) on the two branches show
that both `div(u-alpha_i)` and `div(v-beta_j)` are supported only on the
same shared prime (6).  Their two rows in the isomorphism (5) would be
proportional, contradicting injectivity.

We can now read (4) term by term.  Suppose some vertical vertices are
inactive.  Then no horizontal vertex is inactive.  A constant edge from
an inactive vertical vertex is counted once in the lower bounds (8), at
its active horizontal endpoint.  A constant edge between two active
vertices is counted twice.  Consequently

```text
sum delta_i + sum epsilon_j
 >= #E_0 + #(constant active-active edges).
```

Comparison with (4) proves simultaneously that

* there is no constant active-active edge;
* every `d_ij` at a nonconstant node is one;
* equality holds in every instance of (8).

If there is a constant edge but no inactive vertex at all, the same
argument counts every such edge twice and contradicts (4).  We have proved
the promised orientation statement (1): if constant nodes exist, all of
them are precisely the complete rows belonging to the inactive vertices
on one side of the rectangle.  If none exist, this is the already known
nonconstant-grid case.

Henceforth interchange the variables if necessary and write

```text
I={inactive vertical vertices},      t=#I>=1.         (9)
```

Then every vertical vertex outside `I` has generic curve `A1`, while every
horizontal generic curve has

```text
epsilon_j=t.                                         (10)
```

## 3. Exact form of the restrictions

For `i in I`, (7) already gives

```text
g_i=lambda_i v+mu_i.                                 (11)
```

For `i notin I`, its generic curve is `A1`.  Moreover every node on this
row is nonconstant of degree one.  Abhyankar--Moh--Suzuki makes `g_i` a
plane coordinate, and the coordinate compatibility lemma applied at any
node yields

```text
g_i=rho_i w+P_i(v),            rho_i!=0.             (12)
```

We next claim that, for every horizontal vertex,

```text
q_j=A_j(u)w+Q_j(u),
A_j=rho_j product_(i in I)(u-alpha_i)^(e_ij),
rho_j!=0, e_ij>=1.                                  (13)
```

First assume `t>=2`.  On the generic curve `E_j`, the functions
`u-alpha_i`, `i in I`, are independent units.  Equality in (8) and (10)
shows that its smooth completion is `P1` and that it has exactly `t+1`
points at infinity.  Extend `u:E_j->A1` to a map `P1->P1` of degree `N`.
All preimages of the `t` values `alpha_i` and of infinity lie among those
`t+1` boundary points.  Riemann--Hurwitz therefore gives

```text
(t+1)(N-1) <= 2N-2.
```

Since `t>=2`, this forces `N=1`.  But `N` is the degree in `w` of the
generic equation `q_j-c`; hence `deg_w q_j=1`.  Its `w`-coefficient
vanishes at every inactive `alpha_i` and at no active `alpha_i`.  It has
no other root either, since such a root would give one more independent
unit and contradict (10).  This proves (13).

It remains to justify (13) when `t=1`; write `I={i_0}` and `alpha=alpha_i0`.
Now the generic `E_j` is `G_m`, and the nonconstant unit `u-alpha` is a
monomial in a parameter on `G_m`.  Thus the projection to
`G_m,u-alpha` is etale.  Let

```text
gamma_j=q_j(alpha,w).
```

The values `gamma_j` are pairwise distinct as `j` varies, because (11) is
affine-linear in `v`.  At level `gamma_j`, the only constant node of that
value on this row is the shared prime (6).  Every other vertex contributes
one boundary prime.  The rank count in (5) forces

```text
q_j-gamma_j=(u-alpha)^e H,                            (14)
```

where `H` is irreducible and occurs with exponent one.  (In the remaining
two-row block, the inactive row has only the shared column; unimodularity
then makes the exponent of the private column equal to one.)

In the Laurent plane `k[(u-alpha)^(+-1),w]`, an irreducible factor of
`partial_w q_j` cannot dominate the `q_j`-line, because the generic
projection just proved etale.  It must therefore divide some level
`q_j-c`.  For `c!=gamma_j` that level is irreducible by the divisor
argument, and for `c=gamma_j` equation (14) leaves only `H`.  In either
case association is impossible because differentiation strictly lowers
the `w`-degree.  Hence

```text
partial_w q_j=rho_j (u-alpha)^e,
```

and integration proves (13).  This is exactly the one-puncture Laurent
derivative argument from the monomial crossing, now derived from the
coupled grid lattice.

## 4. The global two-stage affine-plane fibration

Identify

```text
B=R/(f).
```

Consider `Spec B -> A1_u`.  At a root `u=alpha_i` of `a`, its fiber is

```text
k[v,w,x]/(g_i).
```

It is `A2`: use (11) to eliminate `v` when `i in I`, and (12) to eliminate
`w` otherwise.

Fix now `c` which is not a root of `a`.  The `u=c` fiber is

```text
S_c=Spec k[v,w,x]/(a(c)b(v)x+G(c,v,w)).               (15)
```

Project it to `A1_v`.  Off the roots of `b`, eliminate `x`.  At
`v=beta_j`, equation (13) becomes

```text
A_j(c)w+Q_j(c)=0.
```

The coefficient is nonzero: the only roots of `A_j` are inactive
`alpha_i`, and `c` is not any root of `a`.  Thus every geometric fiber of
`S_c->A1_v` is `A1`.  Its defining polynomial is primitive as a linear
polynomial in `x`: at every factor of `b`, the constant term is a nonzero
affine-linear polynomial in `w`.  Hence `S_c` is a domain and is flat over
the PID `k[v]`.  Relative smoothness follows from `partial_x` off `V(b)`
and from the nonzero `partial_w=A_j(c)` on `V(b)`.  The affine-line
fibration theorem, followed by `Pic(A1)=H^1(A1,O)=0`, gives

```text
S_c=A2.                                              (16)
```

The identical argument over `k(u)` proves that the generic `u`-fiber is
`A2`.  Since `B` is a domain, it is torsion-free and hence flat over
`k[u]`.  Thus `B` is an affine-plane fibration over `A1_u`.  Sathaye's
DVR theorem makes it locally polynomial of relative dimension two, and
Bass--Connell--Wright identifies it with the symmetric algebra of a
rank-two projective `k[u]`-module.  That module is free.  Therefore

```text
B=k[u]^[2]=k^[3].
```

Finally the original equality `R=B[f]` says that a polynomial basis of
`B`, together with `f`, is a polynomial basis of `R`.  Hence `f` is a
coordinate.  QED.

## 5. What the combinatorics rules out

The exact obstruction furnished by (4), (5), and (8) is worth recording.
At a constant node, each nonconstant branch consumes one unit-rank (and
hence one Euler-deficit) on its generic curve.  The total available
deficit is only one per constant node.  Therefore a constant node must
have an inactive endpoint.  The coupled divisor lattice forbids both
endpoints from being inactive.  Since inactivity makes every node on that
row constant, the pattern must be a disjoint union of complete rows all
on one side.  This is why arbitrary powers of the rectangular factors do
not create new higher-jet obstructions: after the orientation step, the
quotient is controlled fiberwise and no absorption into `a(u)b(v)x` is
needed.

## 6. The graph statement for a general rational SNC divisor

The orientation argument itself is not special to a rectangle.  Let
`D=union_(nu=1)^N D_nu` be a connected reduced plane divisor whose
components are embedded affine lines, with only transverse double points
and no triple points.  Allow arbitrary positive multiplicities in the
coefficient of `x`, and retain `R=B[f]`.  Label an edge constant when the
restriction of `G` at that node is constant in `w`.

Exactly the same proof gives the following necessary structure.

**Rational-SNC orientation proposition.** There is an independent set
`I` of vertices of the incidence graph such that the constant edges are
exactly the edges incident with `I`.  On a vertex in `I`, the restricted
pencil is affine-linear in a coordinate of that `A1`; on a vertex outside
`I`, if `t_nu` is the number of neighbours in `I`, then its generic curve
has Euler deficit exactly `t_nu`.  Every nonconstant node restriction has
degree one.

Indeed, for `N` vertices and `E` edges the generic Euler calculation is

```text
sum_nu delta_nu + sum_(nonconstant edges e)(d_e-1)
  = #(constant edges).                               (17)
```

An active vertex incident with `t_nu` constant edges has `t_nu`
independent node-parameter units, so `delta_nu>=t_nu`.  An inactive vertex
has an affine-linear pencil and makes all its incident edges constant.
Two adjacent inactive vertices are excluded by the two proportional rows
in the all-level boundary lattice, just as in Section 2.  Therefore each
constant edge is counted at least once in the sum of the active-vertex
lower bounds; an active-active constant edge is counted twice.  Equation
(17) forces exact equality, proving the proposition.

For a rectangular graph this proposition is stronger than it first
appears.  An independent set in a complete bipartite graph lies entirely
on one side, so it gives the parallel-row form (1), and the second affine
coordinate supplies the global two-stage fibration of Section 4.  For a
general rational SNC graph the proposition is the exact combinatorial
output, but a global morphism replacing the rectangular projection need
not exist; that geometric step is genuinely additional.

## 7. Adversarial audit of the four delicate steps

### 7.1 Inactive means affine-linear

If `v` is constant on the geometric generic fiber, then `v` is algebraic
over `k(g_i)` in `k(v,w)`.  Were `g_i` to involve `w`, the elements
`v,g_i` would be algebraically independent.  Thus `g_i=P(v)`.  For generic
closed `c`, `P(v)=c` is a union of `deg P` affine lines; its proved
irreducibility forces `deg P=1`.  This fully justifies (7).

### 7.2 The unique private factor when `t=1`

At `c=gamma_j`, the inactive row contributes only the shared prime.  Every
other vertex contributes exactly one private prime: active vertical rows
have no constant nodes, and the other horizontal rows have distinct
constant values.  If

```text
q_j-gamma_j=(u-alpha)^e product_(ell=1)^k H_ell^(d_ell),
```

the boundary-prime count is `(r+s-2)+1+k`; (5) makes it `r+s`, hence
`k=1`.  After removing the unrelated one-row blocks, the valuation matrix
has the triangular block

```text
[ A  0 ]
[ C  d ].
```

Unimodularity and positivity force `A=d=1`.  The Cartier-divisor length
identifies `d` with the exponent of `H`.  The shared exponent `e` may be
arbitrary, exactly as claimed.

### 7.3 The Riemann--Hurwitz inequality

For `t>=2`, equality in the unit-rank bound gives a completion `P1` with
exactly `t+1` boundary points.  If `u:P1->P1` has degree `N`, all preimages
of the `t` values `alpha_i` and of infinity lie in that boundary.  If `M`
is their total number of distinct points, `M<=t+1`; their ramification
contribution is

```text
(t+1)N-M >= (t+1)(N-1).
```

Riemann--Hurwitz bounds this by `2N-2`, forcing `N=1`.  Irreducibility of
`q_j-T` identifies `N` with `deg_w q_j`, so (13) follows without a hidden
generic-leading-coefficient assumption.

### 7.4 Sathaye and Bass--Connell--Wright hypotheses

The map `k[u]->B=R/(f)` is injective by comparison of `x`-degrees.  Since
`B` is a domain it is torsion-free, hence flat over the PID `k[u]`, and it
is finitely presented.  Section 4 proves all closed fibers are `k^[2]`;
the same inner argument over `k(u)` proves the generic fiber is
`k(u)^[2]`, also after residue-field extension.  Therefore every
localization at `(u-c)` satisfies exactly Sathaye's DVR hypotheses and is
`k[u]_(u-c)^[2]`.  Finite presentation spreads these isomorphisms; the
Bass--Connell--Wright locally-polynomial theorem gives
`B=Sym_(k[u])(P)`, and the rank-two projective `P` is free over the PID.
No smoothness, section, or total-space polynomiality is being silently
assumed in this last step.
