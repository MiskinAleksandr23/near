# Minimal slices and the defect of the top homogeneous kernel

Let

\[
R=k[x_1,\ldots,x_4],\qquad 0\ne D\in\operatorname{LND}_k(R),
\qquad B=\ker D,
\]

and suppose that `D` has a slice.  Give `R` its ordinary degree
filtration.  Write

\[
D=D_0+\cdots+D_d
\]

where the coefficients of `D_i` are homogeneous of degree `i`, and put
`delta=D_d`.  The initial algebra of the invariant ring is

\[
\operatorname{in}(B)
 =\{\operatorname{in}(b):0\ne b\in B\}\cup\{0\}
 \simeq \operatorname{gr} B\subset R.
\]

Here the filtration on `B` is the one induced from `R`.  Notice that
this initial algebra need not be generated by the initial forms of a
chosen finite generating set of `B`.

## 1. The strict degree-reduction move

**Lemma.**  Choose a slice `s` of least ordinary degree among all slices
of this fixed derivation `D`, and write `s_m=in(s)`.  If

\[
d+m-1>0,
\]

then

\[
s_m\in\ker\delta\setminus\operatorname{in}(B).
\]

**Proof.**  The unique contribution of degree `d+m-1` to `D(s)` is
`delta(s_m)`.  Since `D(s)=1` and this degree is positive,
`delta(s_m)=0`.

If `s_m` belonged to `in(B)`, there would be a `b in B` of degree `m`
with `in(b)=s_m`.  Then

\[
D(s-b)=1,\qquad \deg(s-b)<m,
\]

contrary to the choice of `s`.  QED.

Thus the required degree-lowering operation is completely explicit:
lift `in(s)` to an invariant `b` with the same initial form and replace
`s` by `s-b`.  No assertion that an arbitrary invariant of `delta`
lifts is legitimate.

Two immediate consequences are useful.

**Kernel-defect corollary.**  If `d>=1` and `D` has a slice, then

\[
\operatorname{in}(\ker D)\subsetneq\ker(D_d).
\]

Indeed a least-degree slice exists and has degree at least one, so the
lemma supplies an explicit element of the difference.

**A sufficient class.**  A slice LND satisfying

\[
\operatorname{in}(\ker D)=\ker(D_d)
\]

has `d=0`, hence is a translation after a linear change of coordinates.
More generally, the same conclusion holds if `d>=1` and
`in(s) in in(ker D)` merely for one least-degree slice `s`.

This criterion does not assume that `D` has rank at most three.  It says
that a hypothetical rank-four slice LND must have a genuinely
non-exact degeneration of invariants at infinity.

There is a slightly weaker variant which can sometimes be easier to
check.  Put `A=in(B)`.  If `trdeg_k A=3`, then `s_m` is algebraic over
`Frac(A)`, because `A[s_m]` lies in the three-dimensional ring
`ker(delta)`.  Hence a rank-four candidate necessarily violates at
least one of

1. `trdeg A=3`;
2. `A` is relatively algebraically closed in `R` (in the strong sense
   that every element of `R` algebraic over `Frac(A)` belongs to `A`).

In particular, it is enough that `trdeg A=3` and `A` itself be the
kernel of a derivation of `R`: derivation kernels are relatively
algebraically closed in characteristic zero.

For the proposed global minimization, first conjugate `D` by an ambient
automorphism so that its coefficient degree (and then any chosen
secondary leading complexity) is minimal, and then choose a
least-degree slice.  If the rank is four, the affine-slice lemma gives
`d>=2`, while the unimodular-gradient quadratic result gives `m>=3`.
(A coordinate slice would itself force rank one: from `R=B[s]`,
reduction modulo the coordinate `s` gives `B=k^[3]`.)
The displayed kernel defect is therefore unavoidable in that globally
minimal presentation as well.

## 2. Why `in(s) in ker(delta)` alone cannot lower degree

The Anick coordinate gives a sharp audit example.  Put

\[
\Delta=xw-yz,\quad
u=x+z\Delta,\quad v=y+w\Delta,
\]

and

\[
D=(1-zw)\partial_x-w^2\partial_y.
\]

Then `(u,v,z,w)` is a coordinate system, `D=partial_u` in that system,
and

\[
B=\ker D=k[v,z,w].
\]

Every slice of this fixed `D` is `u+b(v,z,w)`.  Its least possible
ordinary degree is three.  To see this, filter `k[v,z,w]` by giving
`v,z,w` weights `3,1,1`.  The leading substitution

\[
v\longmapsto w\Delta,qquad z\longmapsto z,qquad w\longmapsto w
\]

is injective (the image `w(xw-yz)` is transcendental over `k[z,w]`).
Consequently no hidden cancellation lowers the weighted leading degree.
A polynomial `b(v,z,w)` capable of cancelling the cubic part `zDelta`
of `u` would therefore have cubic part

\[
c\,w\Delta+p_3(z,w).
\]

This cannot equal `-zDelta`: comparison of the coefficient of `x`
would require `c w^2=-zw`.  Thus `u` is a least-degree slice for this
fixed field.

On the other hand,

\[
\delta=D_2=-w(z\partial_x+w\partial_y),
\qquad
\ker\delta=k[z,w,\Delta],
\]

whereas the injective leading substitution above gives

\[
\operatorname{in}(B)=k[z,w,w\Delta].
\]

Accordingly

\[
\operatorname{in}(u)=z\Delta
 \in\ker\delta\setminus\operatorname{in}(B).
\]

So even for a slice which is minimal for its fixed derivation, knowing
only that its leading form is killed by the top homogeneous LND gives
no reduction.  The missing datum is precisely liftability from
`in(B)`.  This example does not contradict a conjecture based on global
ambient minimization: the automorphism `(x,y,z,w) -> (u,v,z,w)` makes
the field constant.  It shows that any use of global minimality must
produce an invariant lift (or another explicit conjugating
automorphism); slice-minimality by itself is insufficient.

## 3. Exact frontier left by this argument

For a globally minimal hypothetical rank-four pair `(D,s)`, put

\[
A=\operatorname{in}(\ker D),\qquad \delta=D_{\rm top},
\qquad h=\operatorname{in}(s).
\]

Then necessarily

\[
A\subsetneq\ker\delta,\qquad h\in\ker\delta\setminus A,
\qquad d\ge2,\quad \deg s\ge3.
\]

If `trdeg A=3`, the extension `A subset A[h] subset ker(delta)` is
algebraic at the level of fraction fields, but the Anick calculation
shows that denominators along a boundary component can obstruct the
lift: there `zDelta=(z/w)(wDelta)`.  Therefore an affirmative next lemma
must control saturation of `in(B)` in `ker(delta)` (or use global
minimality to eliminate precisely such boundary denominators).  Equality
of the two kernels, or relative algebraic closedness of the initial
kernel, is already a rigorous sufficient class beyond the known
rank-at-most-three case.
